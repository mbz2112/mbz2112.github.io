Navigating Research Opportunities App Design

Helping students at Columbia University find research opportunities to assist in their professional development
Three other undergraduate students and I worked together to design a user-centric solution to help students explore research opportunities at Columbia University. We decided to focus on students that are interested in pursuing research, either as a career or desire to learn more about a specific topic.
This project was part of a 2-week design sprint hosted by the student organization, Design @ Columbia, and we were given the following prompt to build from: “Your school wants to help students graduate with a better understanding of their career options and interests. Design an experience that helps students explore opportunities for careers.”

Research Questions

  • How might we help students interested in research understand expectations (prerequisites, work culture, funding or credit, work culture, etc.) and learning trajectories for each research position?
  • How might we build a community to help prospective research students connect with current student researchers?

    • Competitor Analysis

    At this time, we were not aware of any centralized resources that students could use to explore research opportunities besides the Columbia University and Barnard College research websites. We started our research by exploring potential competitors, more specifically, what platforms Columbia and Barnard students currently use to explore research opportunities, learn about potential professors to conduct research with, and find related jobs. We decided to assess the five competitors we established based on the following criteria: communication style, layout, features/function, content/information, accessibility, target user group, and potential to build community.     Interviews

    To better understand our user’s needs and goals, we conducted interviews with undergraduate students at Columbia University who are interested in exploring research opportunities. The main concerns are expressed in the following quotes:

    “I wish I could have entered a lab earlier, maybe there should be a better way to apply, instead of attending the [Computer Science] Research Fair and emailing professors.”

    “The harder part [of finding research] was going through the process of getting all the paperwork ready, actually finding the internship I was interested in, but at the same time covered my skill sets.”

    “My interaction with undergraduates interested in research is limited...so maybe something that would be cool would be to have an opportunity to connect with people outside the program.”

    Based on our interviews, we divided the concerns into three main categories to futher explore
    1. Finding research opportunities
    2. Connecting with others doing research
    3. Finding jobs that are compatible with their skill set

    Research Synthesis and User Testing

    Based on our interview data, we created personas to summarize and better understand our user’s end goals. In the following personas, we included the user’s goals, motivations and frustrations that can be used to help drive our system design.
    In addition, I collaborated with another team member on a journey map, where we mapped out various experiences and emotions users have along the way, as well as opportunities to proceed with based on the challenges at each stage.

    Prototyping

    Based on the qualitative data collected from the interviews and summarized in the user personas, as well as the journey map with various opportunities to build from, we each sketched potential solutions. We did an exercise called “Crazy 8’s,” which is an excellent brainstorming exercise that requires each person to sketch 8 unique ideas in 8 minutes. After presenting our “Crazy 8’s” to each other, we discussed what we wanted to bring to our solution sketches. Based on these rough sketches, I collaborated with another team member on a low fidelity prototype of an app students looking for research opportunities could use. The main functions of the app are outlined as follows:
  • Explore and learn about the various research labs on campus
  • Have access to reviews from previous and current student researchers
  • Reach out to student researchers to build community
  • Receive customized recommendations about research opportunities
  • Find the most up to date postings

    User Testing

    After creating a low-fidelity prototype, the next step was to test it out on our target users, students looking for research opportunities, in order to ensure it is easy to navigate, user friendly, and addresses their goals. Each team member conducted one user test, so we had a total of four user tests.
    In preparation for our user tests, we created a series of user stories to help us understand what value each page in our prototype could have for our end users. The table below consists of a series of user stories, corresponding with specific pages or features. This helped us think about what action this page helps the user accomplish and what it helps them accomplish.
    Using the tables above as inspiration, we consolidated our user stories into six main categories, which we used as a guide for when we conducted user tests. The left column consists of the user stories and the right column consists of observations corresponding to each participant (P1, P2, P3, P4), mainly noting whether this feature allowed them to accomplish the goal in the user story and what they found confusing if anything.
    Based on our user tests, we constructed a heat map to consolidate our findings and better understand where our user’s needs are met and where our system design is lacking. The heat map also provides a more visual way to synthesize our findings and understand how important each user story is to our target users. One example is that the heat map shows that 75% of our users are not interested in the first user story: “I want to gain foresight of doing research in academia so that I know what I should prepare in order to pursue this path.” Instead, they are more interested in the user story, “I want to hear about insider insights of a lab so that I can decide if this lab is the best fit.” Thus, the heat map helped us see that our users were more interested in hearing reviews from peers about labs and research experiences than learning how to pursue research as a career path, such as in academia. Knowing this helped us tweak our original prototype to focus more on building community and allowing users to read reviews from others (more like Yelp for reviewing restaurants or Rate My Professor for reviewing college professors). In summary, this heat map helped us gain insight on what aspects to focus on going forward.

    High-Fidelity Prototype

    Based on our user tests, we were able to discuss ways to modify our low-fidelity prototype to better meet the needs of our end users. The modifications we decided to make are summarized as follows:
  • Simplify prototype - our original design was not the most usable as users were confused how to navigate through pages of the app
  • Redesign and reorganize the toolbar at the bottom of the page - we reduced our design to three icons instead of six to allow for simpler navigation
    • Our design consists of three main features summarized as follows
  • Home: recommendations of potential labs, reviews organized by department, and a search icon to take the user to a new page.
  • Chat: allows users to reach out to those who have written reviews and make connections with other student researchers.
  • Me: profile page that covers the user's basic information and activity, including a survey they can fill out for customization, labs they marked as their favorites, and reviews they wrote
